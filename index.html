What is Baking?

Baking is the process of cooking food using dry heat inside an oven. Unlike frying or boiling, baking depends on controlled heat to transform ingredients into cakes, cookies, bread, pastries, and desserts.

Baking is often called a science because every ingredient performs a specific function. Even small changes in measurements or temperature can affect the final result.

Once you understand the role of each ingredient and follow the correct procedures, you can consistently produce delicious baked goods.

Why Learn Baking?

Learning to bake offers many benefits.

  • Save money by making cakes at home.
  • Start a profitable business.
  • Create personalized cakes for celebrations.
  • Develop a valuable lifelong skill.
  • Express creativity through cake decoration.

Many successful bakery owners started by learning one simple cake recipe before expanding their skills.

Understanding the Baking Process

Every successful cake follows the same basic process:

  1. Prepare your ingredients.
  2. Measure accurately.
  3. Mix correctly.
  4. Prepare the baking pan.
  5. Bake at the correct temperature.
  6. Cool completely.
  7. Decorate if desired.

Skipping or rushing any of these steps can affect the final product.

Essential Baking Equipment

You do not need an expensive kitchen to begin baking. Start with basic tools.

Mixing Bowls

Use at least three bowls: Small bowl for eggs; Medium bowl for dry ingredients; Large bowl for mixing batter. Stainless steel bowls are durable and easy to clean.

Measuring Cups

Measuring cups ensure accurate quantities. Dry measuring cups are used for flour, sugar, cocoa powder. Liquid measuring cups are used for milk, water, oil. Never interchange them.

Measuring Spoons

Used for small quantities such as baking powder, vanilla, salt, baking soda.

Kitchen Scale

A digital kitchen scale provides the most accurate measurements. Professional bakers prefer weighing ingredients rather than measuring by volume because it produces consistent results.

Whisk

A whisk combines ingredients while incorporating air into the mixture. It is useful for eggs, cream, cake batter, sauces.

Rubber Spatula

Used to scrape every bit of batter from the bowl without wasting ingredients.

Electric Mixer

Although cakes can be mixed by hand, an electric mixer saves time and creates lighter batters.

Cake Pans

Popular sizes include 6-inch round, 8-inch round, 9-inch round, square pans, loaf pans. Always grease the pan before use.

Cooling Rack

After baking, cakes should cool on a rack so air can circulate around them. This prevents soggy bottoms.

Understanding Baking Ingredients

Every ingredient has a purpose.

Flour

Flour forms the structure of the cake. Cake flour produces softer cakes. All-purpose flour is suitable for most beginner recipes.

Sugar

Sugar does more than sweeten. It also helps cakes brown, keeps cakes moist, improves texture, increases shelf life.

Eggs

Eggs perform several functions. They bind ingredients together, add richness, help cakes rise, improve color, increase moisture. Always use fresh eggs.

Butter

Butter provides rich flavor, moisture, tender texture. Softened butter creams more easily with sugar.

Vegetable Oil

Oil creates softer cakes that remain moist for longer. Many recipes combine butter and oil to achieve both flavor and softness.

Milk

Milk contributes moisture, flavor, better browning, softer crumb.

Baking Powder

Baking powder releases gas during baking, helping cakes rise. Always check the expiry date before use.

Baking Soda

Baking soda requires an acidic ingredient such as buttermilk, yogurt, lemon juice, or vinegar to activate.

Vanilla Extract

Vanilla enhances the flavor of almost every cake. A small amount greatly improves aroma.

Kitchen Measurements

Accurate measurement is the foundation of consistent baking. Use dry measuring cups for flour, sugar and cocoa; liquid cups for milk, water and oil; measuring spoons for small amounts; and a digital kitchen scale for the most reliable results. Professional bakers prepare everything before mixing. This practice is called mise en place, a French term meaning "everything in its place." Before starting: Read the recipe completely; Gather all ingredients; Measure each ingredient; Prepare baking pans; Preheat the oven; Wash and dry your hands; Clear the work surface. Understanding Oven Temperature

Most beginner cakes bake between 170°C and 180°C. Avoid opening the oven door during the first 20 minutes, as this can cause the cake to collapse.

Common Beginner Mistakes
Overmixing

Mix only until ingredients are combined. Excessive mixing develops gluten, resulting in a dense, tough cake.

Incorrect Measurements

Never estimate ingredient quantities. Use proper measuring tools or a kitchen scale.

Expired Raising Agents

Old baking powder or baking soda will prevent cakes from rising properly.

Baking at the Wrong Temperature

An oven that is too hot can burn the outside while leaving the center uncooked.

Removing the Cake Too Early

Test for doneness by inserting a skewer into the center. If it comes out clean or with only a few dry crumbs, the cake is ready.

Frequently Asked Questions
Do I need an expensive oven to start baking?

No. Most beginner recipes bake successfully in a standard home oven set between 170°C and 180°C. An oven thermometer helps you confirm the true temperature.

Can I use all-purpose flour instead of cake flour?

Yes. All-purpose flour is suitable for most beginner recipes. Cake flour produces softer cakes but is not required to begin.

Why did my cake collapse?

Common causes include opening the oven door too early, underbaking, overmixing, or expired raising agents. Follow the recipe and test for doneness before removing the cake.

How do I know when a cake is done?

Insert a skewer into the center. If it comes out clean or with a few dry crumbs, the cake is ready. If wet batter remains, bake for a few more minutes.

Should I use a kitchen scale?

Yes. Weighing ingredients provides the most consistent, repeatable results and is the method preferred by professional bakers.

Your First Cake

A vanilla sponge cake is one of the easiest and most important cakes every beginner should learn. It forms the foundation for birthday cakes, wedding cakes, celebration cakes, and many decorated cakes. Mastering this recipe will make learning more advanced recipes much easier.

CHAPTER TWO · METHOD
Before You Start
Step 1: Read the Recipe

Read every step before mixing. Many baking mistakes happen because people rush into mixing without understanding the full process.

Step 2: Preheat the Oven

Set your oven to 180°C (350°F). Allow the oven to heat for at least 15 minutes before placing the cake inside. A properly preheated oven helps the cake rise evenly.

Step 3: Prepare the Cake Pan

Grease the inside of the cake pan with butter. Cut baking paper to fit the base. Place the paper inside the pan. Lightly grease the paper. Dust lightly with flour. Tap out excess flour. Your pan is now ready.

Step 4: Measure All Ingredients

Measure everything before mixing. Professional bakers call this mise en place, meaning "everything in its place." Doing this prevents mistakes during mixing.

Step 5: Sift the Dry Ingredients

Into a clean bowl, sift together flour, baking powder, salt. Sifting removes lumps and incorporates air, helping to produce a lighter cake. Set the bowl aside.

Step 6: Cream the Butter and Sugar

Place the softened butter in a large mixing bowl. Beat on medium speed for about 2 minutes until smooth. Gradually add the sugar while mixing. Continue beating for another 4–5 minutes until the mixture becomes pale, light, and fluffy. This stage is called creaming. It is one of the most important steps in cake making because it traps tiny air bubbles that help the cake rise.

Step 7: Add the Eggs

Break each egg into a separate bowl before adding it to the mixture. Add the eggs one at a time. Beat well after each addition. If the mixture begins to look slightly curdled, don't worry. It will smooth out once the flour is added.

Step 8: Add the Vanilla

Pour in the vanilla extract. Mix for about 30 seconds until combined.

Step 9: Combine Wet and Dry Ingredients

Reduce the mixer speed to low. Add one-third of the flour mixture. Mix gently. Add half of the milk. Mix again. Repeat this process until all the flour and milk have been added. Finish by folding the batter gently with a rubber spatula. Do not overmix. The batter should be smooth and thick.

Step 10: Fill the Cake Pan

Pour the batter into the prepared pan. Use the spatula to spread it evenly. Tap the pan gently on the work surface two or three times to release large air bubbles.

Step 11: Bake the Cake

Place the cake pan on the center rack of the oven. Bake for 35–40 minutes. Do not open the oven during the first 25 minutes. Opening the door too early can cause the cake to sink.

Step 12: Check for Doneness

Insert a toothpick or skewer into the center of the cake. If it comes out clean or with a few dry crumbs, the cake is ready. If wet batter sticks to the toothpick, bake for another 5 minutes and test again.

Step 13: Cooling the Cake

Remove the cake from the oven. Allow it to cool in the pan for 10–15 minutes. Run a knife gently around the edges. Turn the cake onto a cooling rack. Allow it to cool completely before decorating. Never apply frosting to a warm cake, as it will melt and slide off.

How to Store Your Cake

Store the cake in an airtight container. At room temperature: Up to 3 days. In the refrigerator: Up to 1 week. In the freezer (well wrapped): Up to 3 months.

What Makes Baking Different?

Many people say baking is a science. This is because every ingredient has a specific job, and every step affects the final product. Unlike cooking, where ingredients can often be adjusted freely, baking requires precision. If you use too much flour, too little liquid, or the wrong oven temperature, the result may not be what you expect. Understanding why ingredients behave the way they do will help you become a more confident baker.

The Four Stages of Baking
Stage 1 – Expansion

As the batter heats up: Air trapped during mixing expands; Water begins to turn into steam; Baking powder or baking soda releases carbon dioxide gas. These gases push the batter upward, causing the cake to rise.

Stage 2 – Setting

As the temperature increases: Eggs begin to coagulate (firm up); Flour forms structure; The cake starts holding its shape. At this point, opening the oven door can cause the cake to collapse because the structure has not fully set.

Stage 3 – Browning

The outside of the cake becomes golden brown due to two natural reactions: Caramelization of sugar; The Maillard reaction between proteins and sugars. These reactions create the delicious aroma, color, and flavor associated with baked goods.

Stage 4 – Cooling

Even after removing the cake from the oven, it continues cooking internally for several minutes. Cooling allows the crumb to stabilize, making the cake easier to slice and decorate.

CHAPTER THREE · INGREDIENT SCIENCE
Understanding Each Ingredient
Flour

Purpose: Flour provides the framework of the cake. When mixed with liquid, proteins in flour form gluten, which gives the cake its structure. Too much gluten produces a tough cake. Too little gluten results in a weak cake that may collapse.

Sugar

Sugar is much more than a sweetener. It helps retain moisture, tenderize the crumb, improve color, increase shelf life, feed yeast in bread making. Without enough sugar, cakes become dry and pale.

Eggs

Eggs perform many functions. They bind ingredients together, add moisture, strengthen structure, help trap air during mixing, improve flavor, add richness, enhance color. Always use fresh eggs at room temperature for the best results.

Butter

Butter provides flavor, tenderness, moisture, creamy texture. Soft butter traps air more effectively during creaming than cold butter.

Oil

Oil coats flour proteins, limiting gluten development. This produces cakes that stay moist for longer than butter-based cakes.

Milk

Milk contributes moisture, flavor, protein, better browning, softer texture.

Baking Powder

Baking powder is a complete leavening agent containing both an acid and an alkali. When mixed with liquid and heated, it releases carbon dioxide gas. These bubbles make cakes light and fluffy.

Baking Soda

Baking soda needs an acidic ingredient to activate. Examples include lemon juice, vinegar, yogurt, buttermilk. Without acid, baking soda leaves an unpleasant taste and does not work effectively.

Salt

Although used in small quantities, salt is essential. It balances sweetness, enhances flavor, strengthens structure. Never leave salt out unless the recipe specifically says so.

Vanilla

Vanilla is a flavoring ingredient. A small amount greatly improves the overall taste of cakes, cookies, and frostings.

Understanding Mixing Methods

Different recipes require different mixing methods.

Creaming Method

Used for butter cakes, vanilla cakes, cupcakes. Steps: 1. Cream butter and sugar. 2. Add eggs. 3. Alternate dry ingredients and liquids. 4. Mix until combined. Produces a soft, fluffy cake.

All-in-One Method

All ingredients are mixed together at once. Advantages: Faster, fewer utensils, easier for beginners. Disadvantages: Slightly denser cake.

Whisking Method

Used for sponge cakes. Eggs and sugar are whisked until very airy before adding flour. Produces very light cakes.

Why Accurate Measuring Matters

Professional bakers weigh ingredients because weight is always consistent. For example: One cup of flour can vary greatly depending on how it is scooped. 250 grams of flour is always 250 grams. A digital kitchen scale helps ensure repeatable results.

The Importance of Oven Temperature

Every oven behaves differently. An oven set to 180°C may actually be hotter or cooler. Using an oven thermometer helps you know the true temperature and avoid underbaking or burning.

Introduction

The butter cake is one of the most popular cakes in the world. It is known for its rich flavor, soft crumb, and firm structure, making it ideal for stacking, frosting, and decorating. Unlike sponge cakes, which rely heavily on whipped eggs for volume, butter cakes get their texture from properly creamed butter and sugar. This process creates tiny air pockets that expand in the oven, producing a light and tender cake. A well-made butter cake should have: A golden-brown crust; A fine, even crumb; A rich buttery aroma; A moist interior; A level surface with minimal cracking.

Step 1 – Prepare the Oven

Preheat the oven to 180°C (350°F). Allow it to heat for at least 15 minutes. Never place cake batter into a cold oven because it will affect the rise and texture.

Step 2 – Prepare the Cake Pan

1. Grease the cake pan with butter. 2. Line the bottom with baking paper. 3. Grease the paper lightly. 4. Dust with flour. 5. Tap out any excess flour. This helps the cake release easily after baking.

Step 3 – Prepare the Ingredients

Measure all ingredients accurately before mixing. Bring the following ingredients to room temperature: Butter, Eggs, Milk. Cold ingredients do not mix evenly and can cause the batter to curdle.

Step 4 – Sift the Dry Ingredients

In a clean bowl, sift together flour, baking powder, salt. Sifting improves texture and removes lumps. Set aside.

Step 5 – Cream the Butter and Sugar

Place the softened butter into a large mixing bowl. Beat on medium speed for about 2 minutes until smooth. Gradually add the sugar. Continue beating for 5–7 minutes until the mixture becomes pale, creamy, and fluffy. Why this step matters: Creaming traps tiny air bubbles inside the butter. During baking, these bubbles expand, making the cake lighter.

Step 6 – Add the Eggs

Crack each egg into a small bowl before adding it to the mixture. Add one egg at a time. Beat well after each addition. If the mixture looks slightly curdled, continue mixing gently. The flour will help bring it back together.

Step 7 – Add Vanilla

Add the vanilla extract and mix briefly until incorporated.

Step 8 – Alternate Dry Ingredients and Milk

Reduce the mixer speed to low. Add: One-third of the flour mixture, half of the milk, one-third of the flour, remaining milk, final portion of flour. Mix only until combined. Finish by folding the batter with a rubber spatula. Do not overmix.

Step 9 – Fill the Cake Pan

A great chocolate cake should be: Moist; Rich in chocolate flavor; Soft and tender; Evenly baked; Easy to decorate. The secret to an excellent chocolate cake lies in balancing the dry and wet ingredients while avoiding overmixing.

Understanding Cocoa Powder

Cocoa powder is made by removing most of the fat (cocoa butter) from roasted cocoa beans and grinding the remaining solids into a fine powder. There are two common types:

Natural Cocoa Powder

Light brown in color; Slightly acidic; Strong chocolate flavor; Often paired with baking soda.

Dutch-Processed Cocoa Powder

Introduction

Red Velvet Cake is one of the most recognizable cakes in the baking world. Its deep red color, soft velvet-like crumb, and mild chocolate flavor make it a favorite for birthdays, weddings, Valentine's Day, and other special occasions. Although it looks like a chocolate cake, Red Velvet contains only a small amount of cocoa powder. Its signature texture comes from the combination of buttermilk, vinegar, baking soda, and careful mixing. A properly baked Red Velvet Cake should be: Soft and moist; Fine-textured; Bright red; Light and fluffy; Slightly chocolate-flavored; Smooth enough for frosting.

The Science Behind Red Velvet Cake

Red Velvet Cake is unique because of the reaction between acidic and alkaline ingredients.

Buttermilk

Buttermilk provides acidity, tenderness, and moisture. It reacts with baking soda to produce carbon dioxide, helping the cake rise.

Vinegar

Although only a small amount is used, vinegar strengthens the chemical reaction between the baking soda and the acidic ingredients. It also enhances the cake's soft texture.

Cocoa Powder

Only a small quantity of cocoa powder is used. Its purpose is not to make the cake taste strongly of chocolate but to add depth of flavor.

Red Food Colour

Food coloring gives Red Velvet Cake its characteristic appearance. Use a high-quality gel food coloring for a richer, more vibrant color without thinning the batter.

Introduction

Fruit cake is one of the oldest and most celebrated cakes in the world. It is commonly prepared for weddings, Christmas, birthdays, anniversaries, and festive occasions. Unlike sponge cakes or butter cakes, fruit cake is rich, dense, and packed with dried fruits, nuts, and warm spices. A good fruit cake should have: A moist crumb; Even distribution of fruits; Rich aroma; Balanced sweetness; Firm yet tender texture; Long shelf life.

Understanding Dried Fruits

Dried fruits add sweetness, texture, and flavor to fruit cakes. Common choices include raisins, sultanas, currants, chopped dates, dried cranberries, chopped dried apricots, mixed candied peel, glacé cherries. You can use one type of fruit or a combination, depending on your preference.

Why Soak the Fruits?

Soaking dried fruits helps them absorb moisture, making them softer and more flavorful. Popular soaking liquids include orange juice, apple juice, pineapple juice, strong brewed tea, grape juice. If using alcohol, ensure it is appropriate for your audience and local laws. For beginners, fruit juice works very well. Soak the fruits for at least 8 hours, preferably overnight.

Understanding Spices

Fruit cakes often include warming spices that enhance flavor. Common spices include cinnamon, nutmeg, mixed spice, cloves, ginger. Use spices sparingly so they complement rather than overpower the fruit.

Introduction

Marble cake is one of the most attractive cakes a beginner can learn to bake. It combines vanilla and chocolate batters in one cake, creating beautiful swirl patterns that make every slice unique. Despite its elegant appearance, marble cake is surprisingly easy to make once you understand the correct technique. A perfect marble cake should have: Even chocolate and vanilla swirls; A soft, moist crumb; Rich vanilla aroma; Balanced chocolate flavor; A light golden crust; Distinct patterns without muddy colors.

The Secret Behind Marble Cake

Many beginners think marble cake requires two completely different cake recipes. It doesn't. You prepare one basic vanilla cake batter and divide it into two portions. One portion remains vanilla, while cocoa powder is added to the other to create the chocolate batter. The two batters are layered alternately in the cake pan and gently swirled together. The key word is gently. Over-swirling will mix the batters completely and eliminate the marble effect.

Never decorate a warm cake. Before decorating: 1. Cool the cake completely. 2. Remove the dome using a serrated knife. 3. Level each layer. 4. Brush away loose crumbs. 5. Chill the cake for 20–30 minutes. A cold cake is much easier to decorate neatly.

Place the first cake layer on a cake board. Spread the filling evenly. Recommended fillings include buttercream, chocolate ganache, cream cheese frosting, fruit filling, jam. Keep the filling approximately 1 cm away from the edge to prevent it from squeezing out when the next layer is added.

Applying a Crumb Coat

A crumb coat is a thin layer of frosting that traps loose crumbs. Steps: 1. Apply a thin layer of buttercream over the entire cake. 2. Smooth gently with an offset spatula. 3. Refrigerate for 20–30 minutes. 4. The cake is now ready for the final coat. Skipping the crumb coat often results in crumbs appearing in the finished frosting.

Applying the Final Coat

Add a generous amount of buttercream. Use an offset spatula to spread it evenly. Rotate the cake turntable while holding a bench scraper against the side. Continue smoothing until the frosting becomes even.

Creating Sharp Edges

Sharp edges are a hallmark of professional cakes. Method: 1. Apply extra buttercream above the top edge. 2. Pull the excess inward using an offset spatula. 3. Rotate the turntable while repeating the process. 4. Continue until the top edge becomes crisp and clean. Patience is more important than speed.

Colouring Buttercream

Use gel food colouring because it produces vibrant colours, does not thin the frosting, and requires only a small amount. Mix colours gradually until you achieve the desired shade.

Introduction

Bread is one of the oldest and most widely consumed foods in the world. It is made by mixing flour, water, yeast, salt, and other ingredients to form a dough, which is then kneaded, allowed to rise, shaped, proofed, and baked. Unlike cakes, bread depends on yeast fermentation rather than baking powder or baking soda for rising. A well-made loaf of bread should have: A soft interior (crumb); A golden-brown crust; An even rise; A pleasant aroma; A light, airy texture; Good shelf life.

The Science of Bread

Bread rises because of yeast, a living microorganism. When yeast is mixed with warm liquid and sugar, it begins to feed on the sugar and produces carbon dioxide gas and small amounts of alcohol. The carbon dioxide becomes trapped in the gluten network formed by the flour, causing the dough to expand and become light. During baking, the yeast becomes inactive, the gases expand further, the gluten structure sets, and the crust forms and browns. This process is known as fermentation.

Essential Bread Ingredients

Flour: Bread flour is ideal because it contains more protein. Yeast: Active Dry, Instant, or Fresh. For beginners, instant yeast is recommended. Water or Milk: Use lukewarm liquid (35–40°C). Sugar: Feeds the yeast, improves browning, adds sweetness, helps retain moisture. Salt: Strengthens gluten and enhances flavor. Never place salt directly on yeast before mixing. Fat: Butter, margarine, or oil softens the bread, improves shelf life, adds flavor, produces a finer crumb.

Understanding Gluten

Gluten forms when flour and water are mixed and kneaded. It acts like elastic, trapping gas produced by the yeast. A well-developed gluten structure gives bread better volume, softer texture, chewier crumb, improved structure.

Kneading Dough

Hand Kneading: Push the dough away with the heel of your hand, fold it back, rotate, repeat for 8–12 minutes. Stand Mixer: Mix on low speed using the dough hook for 6–8 minutes. The dough should become smooth, elastic, and slightly tacky.

First Rise (Bulk Fermentation)

Place the kneaded dough in a lightly greased bowl. Cover with plastic wrap or a clean towel. Allow it to rise until doubled in size. Depending on room temperature, this usually takes 45–90 minutes.

Punching Down

After the first rise, press the dough gently to release excess gas. Do not knead heavily again. This step redistributes the yeast and prepares the dough for shaping.

Method

Milk Bread

Milk bread is softer and richer than white bread. Replace the water with 300 ml warm milk. Add 1 egg. Milk gives the bread a softer crumb and a richer flavor.

Sweet Bread

Ingredients: 1 egg, 1 tablespoon milk. Whisk together and brush lightly over the dough before baking. This produces a shiny golden crust.

Troubleshooting

Bread Did Not Rise — Expired yeast, water too hot, under-proofed. Solution: Fresh yeast, check temperature, allow time. Bread is Too Dense — Insufficient kneading, under-proofing. Solution: Knead until smooth/elastic, double in size. Bread Has a Hard Crust — Overbaking, lack of fat. Solution: Bake until done, brush with butter. Bread Collapsed — Over-proofing, weak gluten. Understanding Frying

Frying is the process of cooking food in hot oil. The ideal frying temperature is between 170°C and 180°C (338°F–356°F). If the oil is too cold, dough absorbs too much oil and becomes greasy. If the oil is too hot, the outside burns quickly while the inside remains raw. Use a kitchen thermometer whenever possible.

Use oils with a high smoke point such as vegetable oil, sunflower oil, canola oil, groundnut oil. Avoid using oil that smokes heavily, has a burnt smell, or has been used repeatedly. Fresh oil produces cleaner flavors.

Classic Chocolate Brownies
Difficulty: BeginnerPrep: 20 minBake: 30–35 minYield: 16 squares
Ingredients
  • 200 g dark chocolate
  • 180 g unsalted butter
  • 250 g granulated sugar
  • 3 eggs
  • 1 teaspoon vanilla extract
  • 120 g all-purpose flour
  • 30 g cocoa powder
  • ½ teaspoon salt
Method

Step 1 Preheat the oven to 175°C (347°F). Grease and line a square baking pan. Step 2 Melt the chocolate and butter together. Allow to cool slightly. Step 3 Whisk the eggs and sugar until smooth. Add the vanilla extract. Step 4 Pour in the melted chocolate mixture. Mix gently. Step 5 Sift together: Flour; Cocoa powder; Salt. Fold into the mixture until just combined. Step 6 Pour into the prepared pan. Bake for 30–35 minutes. A toothpick should come out with a few moist crumbs, not wet batter. Step 7 Cool completely before cutting into squares.

Walnut Brownies

Use the same brownie recipe. Fold in: 150 g chopped walnuts or pecans. The nuts add texture and richness.

Baked Cheesecake
Crust
  • 250 g digestive biscuits (crushed)
  • 100 g melted butter
Filling
  • 500 g cream cheese
  • 150 g sugar
  • 2 eggs
  • 150 ml sour cream
  • 1 teaspoon vanilla extract
  • 1 tablespoon cornflour
Method

Step 1 Mix the biscuit crumbs with melted butter. Press firmly into the base of a springform pan. Chill for 20 minutes. Step 2 Beat the cream cheese until smooth. Add sugar. Mix in eggs one at a time. Add sour cream, vanilla, and cornflour. Step 3 Pour the filling over the chilled crust. Bake at 160°C (320°F) for 50–60 minutes. The center should still have a slight wobble. Step 4 Turn off the oven. Leave the cheesecake inside with the door slightly open for 1 hour. This helps prevent cracking. Step 5 Refrigerate for at least 6 hours, preferably overnight, before serving.

No-Bake Cheesecake
Ingredients
  • 250 g crushed digestive biscuits
  • 100 g melted butter
  • 500 g cream cheese
  • 250 ml whipping cream
  • 120 g icing sugar
  • 1 teaspoon vanilla extract
  • 10 g gelatin (prepared according to package instructions)
Method

Prepare the crust as above. Whip the cream until soft peaks form. Beat the cream cheese with icing sugar and vanilla. Fold in the whipped cream and prepared gelatin. Pour over the crust. Refrigerate for 6–8 hours until firm.

Swiss Roll
Ingredients
  • 4 eggs
  • 120 g sugar
  • 120 g flour
  • 1 teaspoon vanilla extract
  • ½ teaspoon baking powder
Filling

Choose one: Strawberry jam; Chocolate spread; Whipped cream; Lemon curd.

Method

Whisk the eggs and sugar until thick. Fold in the flour and baking powder. Spread the batter thinly in a lined baking tray. Bake at 180°C (350°F) for 10–12 minutes. Turn the sponge onto a clean towel dusted with icing sugar. Roll while warm. Allow to cool. Unroll gently. Spread the filling. Roll again tightly.

Bread Pudding
Ingredients
  • 400 g stale bread (cubed)
  • 500 ml milk
  • 3 eggs
  • 120 g sugar
  • 1 teaspoon vanilla
  • ½ teaspoon cinnamon
  • 50 g raisins (optional)
Method

Soak the bread in milk. Whisk together the eggs, sugar, vanilla, and cinnamon. Combine with the soaked bread. Add raisins if desired. Pour into a greased baking dish.
